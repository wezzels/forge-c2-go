# Creating Your Own Assistant "Child" – Why I Built Goclaw

When you're building on top of AI platforms, you eventually hit a wall: **innovation moves faster than your methodology.**

Both Claude and OpenClaw are incredible tools. Each has strengths. Each has weaknesses. But they share one challenge – the features keep growing. Exponentially. And every new capability pulls the platform in directions that may not align with what *you're* trying to build.

## The Feature Avalanche Problem

I've been researching and designing with these tools for months. Every release brings genuinely useful features. But collectively, they're complicating the design patterns I've spent time refining.

It's not that the features are bad. They're great. They just don't serve *my* use case, and they keep shifting the foundation underneath me.

## The Solution: Freeze Your Own Assistant in Time

Today I finished **goclaw** – my custom assistant "child."

Why "child"? Because it's a derivative. A snapshot. An instance that inherits the core capabilities I need, none of the bloat I don't, and won't change out from under me.

The main OpenClaw 4.5 release went sideways recently. It couldn't handle the commands and tasks I was throwing at it. But goclaw – custom-designed for my workflow, installed out-of-band, frozen to the features I wanted – worked flawlessly.

That's the blessing in disguise. When the parent misbehaves, the child you raised still works.

## Going Forward: Copy First, Build Second

From now on, my approach is simple:

**Before building anything complex on an AI platform, make it copy itself first.**

Create your derivative. Freeze it. Document why you chose that snapshot. Then build on solid ground.

The platforms will keep innovating. Let them. You need stability, not features you didn't ask for.
